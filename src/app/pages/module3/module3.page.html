2.2 Navigate steps, staircases and slopes.

Steps and staircases can be somewhat difficult to navigate. You must take safety precautions to avoid accidents or injuries

  • First, verbally inform the VIP when stairs are coming up. Tell them whether the stairs go up or down.
  • Ask the VIP whether they prefer to keep their hand on the handrail or if they wish to keep in touch with your elbow. If they prefer to go by themselves help them to find the handrail by placing your guiding hand on it.

Most of the time, VIP is perfectly capable of walking by themselves. However, there are times when a sighted guide is required. If you need to guide a VIP somewhere, there are some basic techniques you should follow.

While we predominantly use our sight to orient and navigate our environment, VIP’s cannot. They use their senses with assistance such as:

  • a cane stick (the typical white for blind or red and white stick for deaf- blind) to perceive the nearby environment through “touch”.
  • a guide dog.
  • or the assistance of a person.

2.3 Important things to keep in mind before meeting a VIP.

Never approach a VIP by touching them to make them aware that you are there. Instead, ask the VIP if they would like you to be on your left or right side?

  • Being touched in a public area can be scary, especially if there is some noise around. Initiate the conversation by greeting them by their name and Secret CodeTH - not by touching them.
    • Greet them using their name.
    • Introduce yourself - no matter how well you think they know your voice.
    • If there is someone else other than the two of you, say the name of the person who you are addressing at the beginning of the conversation so that they know you are speaking to them personally.
  • Ask them
    • How would you like me to help you/guide you?
    • Would you like to take my arm?
    • Would you like me to go on your left or right side?

NEVER take their arm without permission as this would reduce their independence and would prevent them from being able to stop if they wish.

It’s good practice to:

  • Match your pace to the person you are guiding.
  • Give verbal instructions when necessary but don’t overdo it.
  • Always speak politely - try to avoid commanding instructions.
  • Pay attention to any possible hazard at the ground level and head height.
  • Say when you are about to leave a conversation or a room.
  • Trying not to use your phone unless necessary. You are the eyes for a VIP on the busy suburban roads.

Guiding someone with a guide dog
Always approach a VIP with a guide dog from the opposite side to the dog. It is also important not to take hold of the dog’s harness or lead - even though you’re guiding someone they will still be using the dog. Some people also prefer for you to walk alongside them without holding your arm or walking in front with the dog following you.

There is also advice on how to guide someone with a guide dog under each section in this booklet.

2.4 Standard position to walk with a VIP

Always make sure the VIP is holding your arm just above your elbow. Never link arms with the VIP.

In addition to the use of their cane, a VIP will be able to better sense changes in the pathways by:

  • Gently grabbing the back of one of your elbows
  • or putting their hand onto one of your shoulders.

Doing so will enable them to follow your body movements and will provide them non-verbal feedback to understand directional changes or obstacles on the way such as steps, inclined paths per time. However, they might get distracted so it’s important to always verbally anticipate and describe any upcoming obstacle on your way.

1. Have the VIP hold your arm just above the elbow.

When walking with a VIP make sure to have your elbow bent at 90 degree. Never have your arm straight when walking with a VIP.

To start walking with a VIP, place the back of your hand on the back of the VIP's hand. This will let him or her figure out where your arm is. Then, the VIP can hold your arm just above the elbow. Remember to always keep the arm the VIP is holding to bent at a 90 degrees angle.

2. Make sure the VIP is ½ step or 1 step behind you.


Make sure you are ½ to 1 step in front of the VIP. Never be more than 1 step away or less than ½ a step from the VIP.

To respond to the environment per time some time is needed for us to decide how to react, the same applies to VIPs, the only difference is that not all of them will be able to see it. That is why it is important for you to be one step or half-step in front of them, to signal what’s coming their way.

The VIP should also be slightly to your left or right side. VIPs may feel more comfortable walking at a slower pace than we are used to. So, ask the VIP if they want to walk faster or slower. Keep up a pace you are both comfortable with.

  • Look out for obstacles. This includes obstacles on the ground, to the side, and anything head height. Instruct the VIP to move over or duck when necessary.
  • Always leave enough room to walk around obstacles.
  • If any obstacle arises at the head height just let the VIP know when to duck it and when the obstacle has ended.

2.5 How to adjust when encountering narrow spaces

If the path is too narrow to walk normally with the VIP, move your arm behind you and let the VIP know that they need to walk in a single file.

In the event you have to walk through a narrow space, like a small hallway or a crowd of people:

  • alert the VIP that a narrow space is coming up
    • Verbally announcing it.
    • and, in case they are holding to the back of your elbow, by lowering your arm.

In addition to this, you will have to adjust how you're walking when you encounter narrow spaces.

  • Move your guiding arm behind your back as the VIP should still keep his or her hand on your arm (just above the elbow).
  • Have the person step behind you to walk in a single file.
  • In case of need, instruct the VIP how to move (e.g. obstacles at head height, steps, kerbs…)
  • Always let the VIP know when the narrow space is ended by bringing your arm back to your side.

2.6 Let the VIP know if you need to switch sides.

When walking through a gate makes sure the VIP is on the hinged side of the door by switching sides with them.

In some scenarios, you may have to switch sides when guiding a VIP. For example, you may encounter an obstacle in your path while walking.

  • Tell the VIP that you need to switch sides. Have him or her pause while walking as it's much easier to switch sides when you are both stationary.

Before moving forward,

  • make sure the VIP is holding one of your arms just above the elbow.
  • Make sure they are a half step to a step behind you, a little to your side.

2.7 Navigating special circumstances.

Make sure to be aware of speeding cars. Immediately make the VIP aware of the car by saying “stop!”

When walking with a VIP, you may encounter a dangerous situation. There may be a speeding car or a loose dog.

In such situations, remember:

  • the blind person cannot see the threat.
  • Do not simply move him or her out of the way. Use your words to say something like, "Stop!" or "Move!"
  • After you've got the VIP out of harm's way, explain the threat.

2.8 Move through doors.

Make sure the VIP is on the hinged side of the gate/door when moving through. If the path is too narrow you may need to walk through the gate/door in a single file.

When guiding a VIP through a door, make sure to take special precautions to ensure everything goes smoothly.

  • Make sure the VIP is on the hinged side of the door. If you need to change sides, do so following the instructions listed above.
  • Explain which way the door opens as you near the door. Open the door and proceed to walk through with the VIP you are guiding. If needed, have the VIP close the door behind them when you both passed through.
  • Get to the edge of the stairs. Allow the VIP to let you know when they are ready to proceed. As they may want a moment to adjust themselves before proceeding up or down the staircase.
  • Walk up or down the staircase, remaining one step ahead of the blind person.
  • Go slowly.
  • When you reach the top or bottom of the stairs, let the VIP know it’s the last step.

2.9 Use caution near kerbs and roads.

Kerbs and roads are very dangerous, even with a guide. When approaching a kerb or road let the person know by saying “kerb up” or a “kerb down”. Slow down considerably so that the person can feel the movement through your arm.

  • Cross the street at the narrowest point, go straight across, rather than at an angle.
  • Warn the person about kerbs by saying "kerb up" or "kerb down."
  • Approach a kerb together.

2.10 Help with seating.

In addition to walking, you may need to help a VIP sit down. Tell them:

  • What type of seat it is, (whether it has arms rests, how high it is and if it has wheels)?
  • which way the chair is facing.
  • Walk up to the chair and place your guiding arm on the chair.
  • Let the VIP know which part of the chair you are touching.
  • If the chair is under a table, let them know this.
  • If the chair has wheels, then hold the chair whilst they sit on it.

Let the VIP move his or her arm down yours, feel the chair, and figure out how to sit down from there.

It is always important to always guide people into their seats and not back them into it.

2.11 Guide a blind person into a car.

If you lead a VIP to their car, offer to help him or her into the car. Make sure you ask if they need assistance before helping.

  • Let them know which way the car is facing and which door you're opening. (E.g. "The car is facing forward and I'm bringing you into the passenger's seat.”)
  • Take your guiding hand and place it on the door handle.
  • Instruct the VIP to feel down your hand to find the handle.
  • From here, you can usually let them get in the car on their own.

However, if it's an unfamiliar car you should place your arm inside the car on the roof. This can help the VIP feel how big the car is, avoiding bumping his or her head. Once they are safely seated, let them close the door.

When helping them to get out of the car you should open the door for them and let them know if there is a gap between the car and the kerb.

2.12 Buses, coaches and trains.

All of these can vary in design a lot so there are no hard rules. The basic principles of the other sections can be used in this situation.

  • Let the person know what kind of gap there is between them and the vehicle.
  • Lead them in a single file on and off and through trains (see Single Step section).

2.13 Escalators and travelators.

Let the VIP know if you are approaching an escalator, lift or travelator and ask them if they are happy to use it. Many people may prefer to avoid them, so it’s good to offer an alternative route. If nothing is available, you can request the escalator to be turned off.

  • If the VIP has a guide dog, find an alternative route to escalators. Never try to carry a guide dog on an escalator.
  • In London, not all underground areas are accessible for people with guide dogs, if you find yourself in this situation ask a member of TfL staff for the most appropriate route.

Not all guide dogs are trained to use escalators so ask if you need to find an alternative route. Never try to carry a guide dog on an escalator.

If you are in London, not all areas of the Underground network are accessible for people with guide dogs. You can guide the person to a member of staff who will know the most appropriate route.

2.15 Turnstiles and ticket barriers

Tell the VIP you are approaching a turnstile or a ticket barrier and move to walk on a single file as described in the previous section, “Single Step”. If the turnstile is high this is not possible. But you can help by describing how to navigate it and talk them through what they need to do.

If you are using an automatic ticket barrier the person you are guiding should go first.

  • Guide them in front of the barrier.
  • Place their ticket on the machine.
  • Take it out while they go through the barrier.
  • You can join them afterwards.
